Basketball is a spatial game. Every possession is a negotiation over territory — who controls which part of the floor, and who converts that control into points.

For most of the league's history, coaches and players operated on intuition and tradition. Certain shots were considered "good" because they always had been. The mid-range pull-up was the hallmark of a skilled scorer. The post-up was how big men dominated. The three-pointer was a desperation move, something you threw up when the clock was dying.

Then the math arrived. And it said something that nobody wanted to hear: almost everything people believed about good shot selection was wrong.

A three-pointer at 33% accuracy generates the same expected points per attempt as a two-pointer made at 50%. This equation — so simple it fits on a napkin — quietly rewired how the smartest front offices in basketball thought about the game. Once you see it, you can't unsee it. And once a few teams acted on it, the rest had no choice but to follow.

"The corner three is the same distance as a wing three but scored differently in every coach's playbook — and yet it converts at rates that rival layups when measured per expected point."

By 2024, the transformation was complete. Mid-range attempts had collapsed. The rim and the arc had taken over the floor. What looked like a stylistic shift was actually a rational response to two decades of accumulated data.

To understand why, three ideas need to be in place.

01

Expected Value

A 3-pointer at 33% accuracy generates 0.99 expected points per attempt. A mid-range two at 42% generates only 0.84. That gap compounds over thousands of possessions — the equivalent of scoring several extra points per game, every game, all season.

02

Shot Quality

Not all threes are equal. An open corner three from a 40% shooter is a fundamentally different proposition than a contested pull-up from 28 feet. "Shot quality" collapses difficulty, defender distance, and shooter skill into a single number — and that number determines whether a shot is worth taking.

03

Spacing

When shooters spread to the perimeter, defenders must follow — tearing open driving lanes to the rim. The threat of the three creates the layup. Spacing isn't a side effect of good offense; it's the mechanism that makes the whole system work.

Armed with these ideas, teams began asking a question nobody had asked systematically before: which zones on the floor actually produce the most points per attempt? The answer pointed to exactly two places — the rim and the corners. The vast territory in between turned out to be a trap.

−52% drop in mid-range attempts, 2004 to 2024

That number — a 52% collapse in mid-range attempts over twenty years — is the clearest single measure of how thoroughly the data reshaped the game. It happened season by season, as teams that embraced the math outperformed those that resisted it, until resistance became untenable.

Zone efficiency

What's striking isn't just where the shots are — it's where they aren't. The mid-range corridor, once the domain of Kobe Bryant's stepback and Dirk Nowitzki's one-legged fade, is now largely abandoned. Not because players can't make those shots. Because better options exist nearly every time.

Individual shot charts show the philosophy. League-wide trend data shows the reckoning. From 2004 to 2024, three-point attempt rate more than doubled — from 17% to 39% of all field goal attempts. That's not a gradual drift. It's a structural transformation, and it accelerated with every passing season as the evidence mounted.

The charts below show two sides of the same phenomenon: the rise of three-point volume on one hand, and the simultaneous collapse of mid-range attempts alongside a surge in rim attacks on the other. These lines don't move together by accident — they're the signature of a league that found a more efficient equilibrium and locked it in.

The corner three — which barely registered as a strategic concept in 2004 — nearly tripled its share of attempts over two decades. Those attempts came directly out of the mid-range, which collapsed from more than a third of all shots to barely a seventh.

The trend isn't uniform. Teams arrived at these conclusions at different speeds, through different paths — and the outliers are the most revealing part of the story.

The Golden State Warriors of 2015–19 didn't just adopt the analytics playbook; they embodied it so completely that they became the template every other franchise tried to copy. Stephen Curry and Klay Thompson stretched defenses to their breaking point, turning corner threes into what amounted to automatic points if the defense lost track of them for even a moment.

The Houston Rockets under Daryl Morey took it further — further than anyone thought was sustainable. In 2018 they essentially eliminated the mid-range by front-office mandate, posting a three-point attempt rate of 45% and winning 65 games. The experiment showed what pure optimization looked like at its outer limit.

And then there's the Denver Nuggets, who won the 2023 championship without a dominant three-point shooting profile. Built around Nikola Jokić's gravitational effect on defenses, their story is the critical counterpoint: the data doesn't mandate specific shot types, it demands efficiency. Jokić found it a different way.

"The league hasn't found a ceiling yet. As long as the three-point line stays where it is and defenders have to respect the arc, the pressure to optimize will keep pushing shot charts toward their current extremes."

Twenty years of data point in the same direction. The math behind shot selection isn't going away, and defenses haven't found a way to neutralize it that doesn't create a different problem somewhere else on the floor.

Basketball is beautiful precisely because it's a game of choices made under enormous pressure, in fractions of a second, in front of twenty thousand people. Data doesn't eliminate those choices. It just shows us — clearly, finally — which ones, on average, tend to work best.
